Cross-Gender Friending Ratio

The CGFR asks whether men's and women's friendship networks include women at similar rates in the same place.

Skip to visualizer Next ↓

The core idea

It shows how separated friendship networks are by gender.

For each location, the CGFR compares the share of women in men's friendship networks with the share of women in women's friendship networks. The visualizer also includes a slider for checking whether the pattern changes when the friendship circle is defined more narrowly or broadly.

How to read it

Values near 1 mean less gender segregation in friendship networks.

A CGFR of 1 means men and women form equal shares of their ties with women. Values below 1 mean men's networks include fewer women relative to women's networks. That gap is the signal the map is showing.

Start with the scale

Darker or higher values point to places where cross-gender friendship networks are less separated.

Use the slider if needed

The main pattern is the ratio. The slider is there for checking whether it changes across narrower or broader friendship circles.

Compare within countries

The paper finds a large share of global regional variation happens within countries, not only between them.

Use care with interpretation

The measure reflects Facebook friendships, so it should be read as evidence about social networks, not a complete measure of society.

Why it matters

The paper links CGFR to broader gender relations.

  1. Across countries, CGFR is strongly related to gender gaps in labor force participation.
  2. Within countries, people in higher-CGFR regions tend to report more equal gender attitudes in survey data.
  3. Inside the United States, county-level differences in CGFR line up with demographic and socioeconomic patterns.
