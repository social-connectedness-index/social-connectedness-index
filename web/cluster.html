About Connected Communities

This tool groups sub-national regions into "clusters" using the strength of Facebook friendship ties between them. Two regions are treated as close when a randomly chosen Facebook user in one is highly likely to be friends with a randomly chosen user in the other.

Under the hood it uses hierarchical clustering: every region starts on its own, and the communities whose residents are most connected are merged step by step until the number of clusters you chose remains. Merges are weighted by population, so communities reflect where people actually are rather than how finely a country happens to be subdivided. No geographic constraint is applied — clusters are usually contiguous, but a strong long-distance tie can place non-adjacent regions together.

More details, including the full data download, are at the Humanitarian Data Exchange.

How the clustering works

This tool finds communities — groups of regions whose residents are unusually likely to be Facebook friends with one another. Everything runs in your browser. Here is the full method, from raw data to the coloured map.

1. The data

For every pair of regions we use the public Social Connectedness Index (SCI): a measure proportional to the probability that a randomly chosen Facebook user in one region and a randomly chosen user in the other are friends. Because it is already divided by each region's user count, the SCI is a per-person measure — it is not inflated simply by a region being large.

2. Distance between two regions

We turn connectedness into a distance: regions with a high SCI are “close”, regions with a low SCI are “far”. The distance is −log(SCI). The log matters because the SCI spans many orders of magnitude; without it a handful of very strong ties would dominate everything. Distances are then scaled to a 0–1 range, and the rare pairs with no SCI reading are filled with a high (but not extreme) value.

3. Building communities

We use hierarchical agglomerative clustering. Every region starts as its own community; we then repeatedly merge the two closest communities, recording the order of every merge, until only one remains.

The crucial choice is how to measure the distance between two communities that each contain many regions. We define it as the average distance between a random resident of one community and a random resident of the other. Each region's contribution is weighted by its population, so the distance between communities I and J is the population-weighted average of the region-to-region distances (each pair weighted by the product of the two regions' populations). This is known as population-weighted average linkage.

Weighting by population matters because administrative regions vary wildly in size. Without it, a country carved into hundreds of tiny districts would out-vote one split into a few large provinces. With it, a community's “pull” reflects its people: populous places anchor communities, and sparse areas are absorbed into their neighbours.

4. Choosing how many communities

Because every merge was recorded, the full hierarchy forms a tree. Asking for K communities just means cutting that tree so K branches remain — which is why the slider responds instantly without re-computing anything.

5. Tidying small fragments

After cutting, any community whose total population is tiny compared with the others is folded into its nearest neighbour, so the map isn't peppered with one-off fragments. A single very populous region (a major metro) is kept even though it is one unit — the test is on people, not on the count of regions. As a result you may occasionally get slightly fewer communities than you asked for.

6. Colours, borders and the animation

Neighbouring communities are given strongly contrasting colours so boundaries are easy to read. There is no geographic constraint in the clustering itself — communities are usually contiguous because connectedness falls off with distance, but a genuinely strong long-distance tie (for example, migration links) can place non-adjacent regions in the same community.

The Animate button sweeps from one community up to thirty, splitting one community at a time: it highlights the community about to split, shows the split, then restores the full colouring — so you can follow exactly how the hierarchy unfolds.
